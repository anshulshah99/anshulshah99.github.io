Anshul Shah

University of California-San Diego ayshah@ucsd.edu

I'm a PhD student studying computer science education and software engineering under Gerald Soosairaj, Soohyun Liao, Bill Griswold, and Leo Porter. Broadly, my research focus is to promote effective software engineering processes among students. I am involved in two avenues of research that relate to this overall goal:

  • First, I'm interested in using learning analytics to understand student development processes (incremental development, test-driven development, debugging, etc) and evaluate pedagogical interventions, such as Live Coding, on such development processes.
  • Second, I'm interested in the self-regulation skills that are required for effective programming processes. I hope to promote the metacognitive skills needed to design, plan, implement, and evaluate software among students by studying the most effective metacognitive activities and methods to incentivize students to engage in such activities.

Projects

Teaching Incremental Software Development through "Live Coding"

Gerald Soosairaj, Bill Griswold, Leo Porter

My project focuses on promoting effective software engineering practices such as frequent testing and incremental coding by utilizing "live coding" during lectures. Live coding is a teaching practice where an instructor shows the entire programming process live in front of a class, from opening a new file, writing a bit of code, testing the code, debugging, and then continuing in this incremental process.

Improving Student Metacognition through CompassX

Soohyun Liao

CompassX is a platform that provides students with the cognitive practice of self-assessment but emphasizes the metacognitive skills of planning, self-monitoring, evaluating, and reflecting. We have deployed the app in multiple courses at UCSD, and are now using it to discover interventions that incentivize students to engage in the metacognitive activities on CompassX.

September 2021 - present

CS1 Reviewer App

Kristin Stephens-Martinez

I created a website for the introductory Python course at Duke University where students could customize their own study quizzes based on a variety of topics and view a summary of their responses in a personalized dashboard. The website estimated student knowledge using the Bayesian Knowledge Tracing algorithm, and used these calculations to provide autogenerated quizzes to students to target the concepts that are more difficult for them.
